1. SVM¶

a. Margin¶

Explain why we can set the margin $c = 1$ to derive an equivalent SVM formulation.

In the optimization problem

$$ \max_{w,b} \frac{2c}{\Vert{w}\Vert} \\ s.t. y_i(w^\top x_i + b) \geq c, \forall i $$

$c$ represents the size of the margin. Its presence in both the maximization problem and the constraint means that it directly scales both $w$ and $b$ and can be treated as a constant in the optimization problem. Therefore, we can set $c = 1$, since the margin size is not being optimized.

b. Weight vector¶

Using Lagrangian dual formulation, show that the weight vector can be represented as

$$ w = \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i y_i x_i $$

where $\alpha_i \geq 0$ are the dual variables. What does this imply in terms of how to relate data to $w$?

The dual formulation is:

$$ L(w, \alpha\, b) = \frac{1}{2}w^\top w + \sum_{i=1}^{m}\alpha_i\left(1 - y_i(w^\top x_i + b)\right) $$

To minimize this, we take the derivative:

$$\begin{align} \frac{\partial L}{\partial w} & = 2*\frac{1}{2}w + \sum_{i=1}^{m} \alpha_i (-y_i x_i ) \\ & = w - \sum_{i=1}^{m} \alpha_i y_i x_i \end{align}$$

then set to 0 and solve for a representation of $w$:

$$\begin{align} w & - \sum_{i=1}^{m} \alpha_i y_i x_i = 0 \\ w & = \sum_{i=1}^{m} \alpha_i y_i x_i \end{align}$$

This final representation of the weight vector $w$ implies that it is a linear combination of $\alpha_i$, $x_i$ (the input data), and $y_i$ (the class assigned during training).

c. Data points on the margin¶

Explain why only the data points on the "margin" will contribute to the sum above, i.e. playing a role in defining $w$. Hint: use the Lagrangian multiplier derivation and KKT condition we discussed in class.

The KKT condition is $a_i g_i(w) = 0$, which can also be written as $a_i\left(1-y_i(w^\top x_i + b)\right) = 0$.

We also have the following facts:

  • $y \in \{-1, 1\}$
  • $w^\top x + b \in \{-1, 1\}$ (the margin edges parallel to the decision boundary)

Therefore, $y_i(w^\top x_i + b) = 1$ for points on the margin, meaning that $1-y_i(w^\top x_i + b) = 0$ for those points. For points off the margin, $1-y_i(w^\top x_i + b) < 0$.

For points on the margin, $\alpha_i > 0$; for those off the margin, $\alpha_i = 0$. Both will satisfy the KKT condition when the terms are multiplied.

However, because $w = \sum_{i=1}^{m} \alpha_i y_i x_i$, any point with $\alpha_i = 0$ (i.e. any point off the margin) will not contribute to the final value of $w$. Therefore, only points on the margin contribute to the sum.

2. Naive Bayes for spam filtering¶

In this problem, we will use the Naive Bayes algorithm to fit a spam filter by hand. This will enhance your understanding of Bayes classifier and build intuition.

Spam filters are used in all email services to classify received emails as "Spam" or "Not Spam". A simple approach involves maintaining a vocabulary of words that commonly occur in "Spam" emails and classifying an email as "Spam" if the number of words from the dictionary that are present in the email is over a certain threshold. We are given the vocabulary consisting of 15 words:

$V = \{$secret, offer, low, price, valued, customer, today, dollar, million, sports, is, for, play, healthy, pizza$\}$

We will use $V_i$ to represent the $i$th word in $V$. As our training dataset, we are also given 3 example spam messages:

  • million dollar offer
  • secret offer today
  • secret is secret

and 4 example non-spam messages:

  • low price for valued customer
  • play secret sports today
  • sports is healthy
  • low price pizza

Recall that the Naive Bayes classifier assumes that the probability of an input $x = [x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n]^{\top}$ depends on its class $y$. In our case, the input vector $x$ corresponding to each message has length $n = 15$ equal to the number of words in the vocabulary $V$, where each entry $x_i$ is equal to the number of times word $V_i$ occurs in $x$.

c. Maximum likelihood estimates¶

In the Naive Bayes model, the likelihood of a sentence with feature vector $x$ given a class $c$ is

$$ \mathbb{P}(x|y=c) = \prod_{k=1}^n \theta_{c,k}^{x_k} $$

where $\theta_{c,k} \in (0,1)$ is the weight of word $k$ in class $c$, which satisfies $\sum_{k=1}^n \theta_{c,k} = 1, \forall c$. Calculate the maximum likelihood estimates of $\theta_{0,1}, \theta_{0,7}, \theta_{1,1}, \theta_{1,15}$ by maximizing $\mathbb{P}(x|y=c)$ with respect to $\theta_{c,k}$ and given data.

Hint: Consider the Lagrangian function for solving this constrained optimization problem. You only need to introduce one Lagrangian multiplier because you only have one constraint. Consider log-likelihood (i.e. taking the log of the cost function). Then solve it from there.

i. Summary¶

The maximum likelihood estimates are:

  • $\theta_{0,1}$ = 0.333, or 3/9
  • $\theta_{0,7}$ = 0.111, or 1/9
  • $\theta_{1,1}$ = 0.067, or 1/15
  • $\theta_{1,15}$ = 0.067, or 1/15

The full derivation and explanation is below.

ii. Derivation¶

In training a Naive Bayes classifier, we want a model $p(y=c|x)$ for class $c$.

By Bayes' theorem:

$$\begin{align} p(y=c|x) = \frac{p(x|y=c) p(y=c)}{p(x)} \end{align}$$

For the purpose of learning the model, we only care about the numerator $p(x|y=c) p(y=c)$ because the denominator is effectively a constant in the training process. Therefore, our problem becomes:

$$\begin{align} p(y=c|x) = p(x|y=c) p(y=c) = p(x,y) \end{align}$$

With the assumption of independence and the chain rule of probability:

$$\begin{align} p(y=c|x) = p(x,y) & = p(x_1, x_2, \dots x_n, y=c) \\ & = p(y=c) \prod_{k=1}^{n} p(x|y=c) \\ & = p(y=c) \prod_{k=1}^{n} \theta_{c,k}^{x_k}, \end{align}$$

where $n$ is the number of features (i.e. 15 - one for each word).

Across all points in the training set, the equation becomes:

$$\begin{align} p(x, y) = \prod_{i=1}^{m} p(y_i=c) \prod_{k=1}^{n} \theta_{c,k}^{x_{i_k}} \end{align}$$

$p(y=c)$ can also be expressed as a combination of the two probabilities: $p(y=1)^c p(y=0)^{1-c}$, and the equation can be expressed in log-space to simplify optimization:

$$\begin{align} log[p(x, y)] & = \sum_{i=1}^{m} log\left[ p(y_i=c) \prod_{k=1}^{n} \theta_{c,k}^{x_{i_k}} \right] \\ & = \sum_{i=1}^{m} log\left[ p(y=1)^c p(y=0)^{1-c} \prod_{k=1}^{n} \theta_{c,k}^{x_{i_k}} \right] \\ & = \sum_{i=1}^{m} log\left[ p(y=1)^c p(y=0)^{1-c} + \sum_{k=1}^{n} log(\theta_{c,k}^{x_{i_k}}) \right] \end{align}$$

The function above is also subject to the constraint $\sum_{k=1}^{n} \theta_{c,k} = 1$. In order to optimize it, we can use the method of Lagrange multipliers. In general, $L(x,\lambda) = f(x) + \lambda g(x) \ s.t. \ g(x) = 0$; in this case, the equality constraint $g(x) = 1$ can be expressed as $g(x) - 1 = 0$. Therefore, the Lagrangian function for constrained optimization is:

$$\begin{align} L[p(x,y)] = \sum_{i=1}^{m} \left( log \left[ p(y=1)^c p(y=0)^{1-c} + \sum_{k=1}^{n} log(\theta_{c,k}^{x_{i_k}}) \right] + \lambda \left[ \sum_{k=1}^{n} \theta_{c,k} - 1 \right] \right) \end{align}$$

In order to maximize, we take the derivative and set it to zero:

$$\begin{align} \frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta_{c,k}} = \sum_{i=1}^{m} \frac{x_k}{\theta_{c,k}} (y=c) + \lambda \end{align}$$

Here, $(y=c)$ is an indicator variable whose value is 1 if $y=c$ and 0 if $y \neq c$.

$$\begin{align} \sum_{i=1}^{m} \frac{x_k}{\theta_{c,k}} (y=c) + \lambda & = 0 \\ \frac{1}{\theta_{c,k}} \sum_{i=1}^{m} x_k (y=c) + \lambda & = 0 \\ {\theta_{c,k}} & = -\frac{1}{\lambda} \sum_{i=1}^{m} x_k (y=c) \end{align}$$

Using the constraint/identity $\sum_{k=1}^{n} \theta_{c,k} = 1$:

$$\begin{align} - \frac{1}{\lambda} \sum_{k=1}^{n} \sum_{i=1}^{m} x_k (y=c) & = 1 \\ - \sum_{k=1}^{n} \sum_{i=1}^{m} x_k (y=c) & = \lambda \end{align}$$

Substituting this back into the formula for $\theta_{c,k}$ returns this expression of the weight $\theta_{c,k}$:

$$\begin{align} \theta_{c,k} = \frac{-\sum_{i=1}^{m} x_k (y=c)}{-\sum_{k=1}^{n} \sum_{i=1}^{m} x_k (y=c)} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{m} x_k (y=c)}{\sum_{k=1}^{n} \sum_{i=1}^{m} x_k (y=c)} \end{align}$$

In plain English, this is equivalent to:

  • The number of occurrences of word $k$ across all data points in class $c$,
  • Divided by the total number of occurrences of all words, across all data points in class $c$

This is fairly intuitive as well: the contribution of each word is proportional to its representation among the sentences that make up a given class.

Using this definition of $\theta_{c,k}$, I calculated the following values:

  • $\theta_{0,1}$ = 0.333, or 3/9
  • $\theta_{0,7}$ = 0.111, or 1/9
  • $\theta_{1,1}$ = 0.067, or 1/15
  • $\theta_{1,15}$ = 0.067, or 1/15

d. Classification¶

Given a new message "today is secret", decide whether it is spam or not spam based on the Naive Bayes classifier learned from the above data.

i. Summary¶

"today is secret" is classified as spam with a joint probability/numerator of ~0.00176 vs. ~0.00017 for non-spam. A more detailed explanation is below.

ii. Explanation¶

From the Bayes' theorem, we have $p(y|x) = \frac{p(x|y)p(y)}{p(x)}$. For assigning a class, we take the $argmax$ of $p(y|x)$: choose the class with the greater value.

For the purposes of classification, only the numerator is important, since $p(x)$ will be identical for each class' model. Therefore, selecting a class only requires comparing the numerators:

  • $p(x|y=0)p(y=0)$
  • $p(x|y=1)p(y=1)$

From part c, we know that $p(x|y=c)$ is defined as $\prod_{k=1}^{n} \theta_{c,k}^{x_{i_k}}$. Therefore, the numerator (which is equivalent to the joint probability $p(x,y=c)$ can be expressed as:

$$ p(y=c) \prod_{k=1}^{n} \theta_{c,k}^{x_{i_k}} $$

After calculating the values of $\theta_{c,k}$ for every word in both classes, the joint probabilities are:

  • 0.00176367 for spam ($y=0$)
  • 0.00016931 for non-spam ($y=1$)

Spam has a greater probability, so the message is classified as spam.
